PROJECT 39: THE SUBSIDIARY THAT NEEDED ACCESS TO HQ'S DATA — Inter-Account S3 Access via Bucket Policy & IAM Role

🔴 HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ ShopNow has expanded internationally
→ A subsidiary company "ShopNow Europe" was established with its OWN AWS account
→ Two completely separate AWS accounts now exist:

EntityAWS Account IDAliasRegion
ShopNow HQ (India)111111111111shopnow-hqap-south-1
ShopNow Europe (Ireland)222222222222shopnow-eueu-west-1

→ These accounts are COMPLETELY ISOLATED by default
→ No resource in Account 222222222222 can see, list, read, or write any resource in Account 111111111111
→ This is the fundamental AWS security model — account boundary is the strongest isolation boundary

The Business Need:

→ ShopNow HQ has a curated data lake in S3 (built across Projects 1, 12, 17, 21, 25)
→ Bucket: shopnow-datalake-curated in Account 111111111111, Region ap-south-1
→ Contains:

  • Product catalog (Parquet files) — products/
  • Pricing data (Parquet files) — pricing/
  • Customer segments (Parquet files) — segments/
  • Historical orders (Parquet files) — orders/

→ ShopNow Europe needs:

  • READ access to products/ and pricing/ prefixes — to display correct products and prices on their European storefront
  • NO access to segments/ — contains HQ-only marketing segmentation data
  • NO access to orders/ — contains Indian customer PII subject to Indian data regulations
  • Their Glue ETL jobs (running in Account 222222222222) need to read this data
  • Their Athena queries (running in Account 222222222222) need to query this data
  • They should NEVER be able to write, delete, or modify anything in HQ's bucket

Current Workaround (Broken):
→ A cron job on an EC2 instance in HQ's account runs nightly
→ It copies products/ and pricing/ data from HQ's bucket to a bucket in Europe's account
→ Problems:

  • Data is always 24 hours stale
  • Copy job fails silently sometimes — Europe works with 48-72 hour old data
  • Doubles storage cost (same data in two buckets)
  • No access audit trail — once copied, HQ loses visibility
  • Copy job uses hardcoded AWS access keys (violation found in Project 37)

The CTO asks:

"Can ShopNow Europe's services read directly from our S3 bucket in real-time,
without copying data, with fine-grained access control,
and with full audit trail of who accessed what?"

→ The answer is YES — using two complementary approaches:

  • Approach A: Cross-account S3 Bucket Policy (resource-based)
  • Approach B: Cross-account IAM Role Assumption (identity-based)
  • Recommended: Combination of BOTH for defense-in-depth

🟡 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ First — understand the TWO fundamentally different ways cross-account access works in AWS

Method 1 — Resource-Based Policy (Bucket Policy):

Account A (HQ) owns the S3 bucket
        ↓
Account A adds a BUCKET POLICY that says:
  "Allow Account B's specific role to perform specific S3 actions"
        ↓
Account B's role can now access Account A's bucket DIRECTLY
        ↓
The role STAYS in Account B — it does NOT "become" an Account A identity
        ↓
Object ownership: Objects uploaded by Account B are owned by Account B
  (unless bucket ownership settings override — important detail)

Method 2 — Identity-Based (Cross-Account Role Assumption via STS):

Account A (HQ) creates an IAM Role with a TRUST POLICY
        ↓
Trust Policy says: "Account B's specific role can ASSUME this role"
        ↓
Account B's role calls STS AssumeRole → gets TEMPORARY credentials for Account A's role
        ↓
Account B's service now ACTS AS Account A's role temporarily
        ↓
All actions are performed AS Account A's identity
        ↓
Object ownership: Objects uploaded are owned by Account A (because the assumed role is in Account A)

Key Differences:

AspectBucket Policy (Resource-Based)Role Assumption (Identity-Based)
Where permission is definedOn the RESOURCE in Account AOn the ROLE in Account A + Trust Policy
Identity of accessorAccount B's original roleAccount A's assumed role (temporary)
KMS encrypted objects❌ Cannot decrypt CMK-encrypted objects across accounts via bucket policy alone✅ Assumed role in Account A can decrypt (if role has KMS permissions)
Object ownershipAccount B owns uploaded objectsAccount A owns uploaded objects
GranularityPer-bucket, per-prefix, per-actionFull IAM policy granularity
ComplexitySimpler for read-only accessMore complex but more powerful
Audit trailCloudTrail shows Account B's role ARNCloudTrail shows assumed role ARN in Account A
Cross-account KMSRequires KMS key policy update separatelyAssumed role inherits Account A's KMS permissions

For ShopNow's scenario — WHY we need Role Assumption (Method 2):
→ HQ's S3 bucket is encrypted with SSE-KMS using shopnow-s3-datalake-key (Project 36)
→ Bucket Policy alone grants S3 access but NOT KMS decryption
→ Europe's role in Account B cannot call KMS Decrypt on Account A's key via bucket policy alone
→ With Role Assumption → Europe assumes a role IN Account A → that role has BOTH S3 AND KMS permissions
→ This is the cleanest, most secure approach

→ The approach:

  • Step 1 — Understand the account topology and trust model
  • Step 2 — Create a cross-account IAM role in HQ's account (Account A)
  • Step 3 — Configure the trust policy to allow Europe's account (Account B)
  • Step 4 — Attach permissions to the cross-account role (S3 + KMS)
  • Step 5 — Configure Europe's roles to assume the HQ role
  • Step 6 — Also configure bucket policy as defense-in-depth
  • Step 7 — Configure KMS key policy for cross-account access
  • Step 8 — Verify the complete flow
  • Step 9 — Set up audit trail

The Flow:

Understanding External ID — The Confused Deputy Problem:

→ Without External ID:

Attacker Account (333333333333)
        │
        │ Attacker creates a role in their account
        │ that assumes CrossAccountReader in 111111111111
        │
        │ If Trust Policy only checks Account ID 222222222222 → BLOCKED ✅
        │
        │ BUT what if the attacker tricks a service in 222222222222
        │ to assume the role on the attacker's behalf?
        │
        │ This is the "Confused Deputy" attack:
        │   Attacker uses a legitimate service in 222222222222
        │   as a proxy to assume the role in 111111111111
        │   The service doesn't know it's being exploited

→ With External ID:

Trust Policy says: "Allow 222222222222 to assume this role
                    ONLY IF ExternalId = shopnow-europe-2025-xacct"
        │
        │ Legitimate Europe services know the External ID → can assume ✅
        │ Attacker doesn't know the External ID → cannot assume ❌
        │ Even if attacker tricks a service in 222222222222 →
        │   the service would need to pass the correct External ID
        │   which the attacker doesn't know → BLOCKED ✅

→ External ID is a shared secret between the two parties
→ It is NOT a security credential — it's a confusion-prevention mechanism
→ It should be unique, non-guessable, and communicated securely between account owners
→ Best practice: Use a combination of organization name + date + random string

STEP 4 — CONFIGURE KMS KEY POLICY FOR CROSS-ACCOUNT ACCESS

→ The cross-account role CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEurope needs kms:Decrypt
→ This role exists IN Account A → so it's a normal key user addition

→ Navigate to KMS → shopnow-s3-datalake-key → Key policy
→ The role was already granted kms:Decrypt in Step 2's IAM policy
→ But the KEY POLICY must also allow this role

Add to Key Policy — Key Users section:

PrincipalActions
arn:aws:iam::111111111111:role/CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEuropekms:Decrypt, kms:DescribeKey

→ This follows the dual-gate model from Project 36:

  • IAM Policy on the role → allows kms:Decrypt ✅
  • Key Policy on the KMS key → allows the role as key user ✅
  • Both agree → decryption works

ALTERNATIVE: If using Bucket Policy approach (without role assumption):
→ Europe's role in Account 222222222222 would need KMS access
→ The KMS key policy would need to add Account 222222222222's role as a principal
→ This is MORE complex because:

  • Key policy must reference an external account's role ARN
  • External role must have kms:Decrypt in its OWN IAM policy
  • AND key policy must allow it
  • Three-way agreement needed (Bucket Policy + IAM Policy + Key Policy)
    → This is why Role Assumption is cleaner — the assumed role is IN Account A, so normal dual-gate applies

STEP 6 — CONFIGURE BUCKET POLICY AS DEFENSE-IN-DEPTH

→ Sign back into Account 111111111111 (ShopNow HQ)
→ Navigate to S3 → shopnow-datalake-curated → Permissions → Bucket policy

Why add a bucket policy when Role Assumption already works?
→ Defense-in-depth — if someone modifies the IAM role's policy to add s3:DeleteObject, the bucket policy still DENIES it
→ The bucket policy acts as a GUARDRAIL independent of IAM
→ Belt AND suspenders approach (same philosophy as KMS key policy + IAM policy from Project 36)

Bucket Policy Configuration:

Allow Cross-Account ReadAllowarn:aws:iam::111111111111:role/CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEuropes3:GetObjectarn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curated/products/*, arn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curated/pricing/*None

Explicit Deny WriteDenyarn:aws:iam::111111111111:role/CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEuropes3:PutObject, s3:DeleteObject, s3:PutBucketPolicy, s3:PutBucketAclarn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curated/*, arn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curatedNone
Explicit Deny Restricted PrefixesDenyarn:aws:iam::111111111111:role/CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEuropes3:GetObjectarn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curated/segments/*, arn:aws:s3:::shopnow-datalake-curated/orders/*None

Why EXPLICIT DENY is critical:

→ AWS policy evaluation: Explicit Deny ALWAYS overrides any Allow
→ Even if someone accidentally adds s3:PutObject to the cross-account role's IAM policy
→ The bucket policy's explicit deny on PutObject will BLOCK it
→ Even if someone adds s3:GetObject for orders/* to the IAM policy
→ The bucket policy's explicit deny on orders/* will BLOCK it

Policy Evaluation Order (Comprehensive):

Request arrives (GetObject on orders/data.parquet)
        ↓
Step 1: Is there an EXPLICIT DENY in any policy?
  → Bucket Policy has Deny on orders/* for this role → YES
  → DENIED immediately — no further evaluation
        ↓
(If no explicit deny, evaluation continues:)
Step 2: Is there an ALLOW in any applicable policy?
  → IAM Policy? Bucket Policy? Both?
  → If Allow found → ALLOWED
  → If no Allow found → IMPLICIT DENY

→ Explicit Deny is the STRONGEST control in AWS — nothing can override it
→ Use it for cross-account guardrails where you want ABSOLUTE restrictions

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Always use Cross-Account IAM Role Assumption for KMS-encrypted resources — bucket policy alone is insufficient
→ Always use External ID in trust policies — prevents confused deputy attacks
→ Always restrict trust policy to SPECIFIC role ARNs — not entire accounts when possible
→ Always add EXPLICIT DENY in bucket policy for actions the cross-account role should NEVER perform (write, delete)
→ Always add EXPLICIT DENY for prefixes the cross-account role should NEVER access (segments, orders)
→ Always use TEMPORARY credentials (STS) — never create IAM users with permanent access keys for cross-account
→ Always configure the cross-account role with MINIMUM necessary permissions — read-only if only reading
→ Always enable CloudTrail in BOTH accounts to audit cross-account access
→ Always use descriptive role names indicating the cross-account purpose and partner
→ Always tag cross-account roles with partner account ID, access level, and allowed resources
→ Always document the External ID securely — share via encrypted channel, not email or wiki
→ Always set session duration appropriately — 1 hour default, increase only if needed

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ Never create IAM users with permanent access keys for cross-account access — use roles + STS
→ Never trust an entire account (root) in trust policy without External ID — confused deputy risk
→ Never grant write permissions in cross-account roles unless absolutely required — read-only by default
→ Never skip bucket policy explicit deny — IAM policies can be modified, bucket policy is the guardrail
→ Never share External ID via unencrypted channels (plain email, wiki, Slack) — treat as sensitive
→ Never grant s3:* or kms:* in cross-account roles — always enumerate specific actions
→ Never use the same cross-account role for multiple partner accounts — one role per partner for isolation
→ Never forget KMS key policy update when S3 is SSE-KMS encrypted — most common cause of cross-account "Access Denied"
→ Never assume bucket policy alone handles KMS — it does NOT (separate authorization)
→ Never grant iam:* or sts:* beyond AssumeRole in cross-account scenarios — prevents privilege escalation
→ Never configure cross-account access without CloudTrail auditing — you lose visibility
→ Never skip prefix-level restrictions in bucket policy — granting access to entire bucket when only specific prefixes needed violates least privilege

🔴 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use cross-account S3 access when data must NEVER leave the source account — use compute in the source account instead
→ Do NOT use cross-account access for data subject to strict data residency regulations that prohibit cross-account data flow — consult legal

MECHANIC 4 — SESSION POLICY (Scoping Down Assumed Role)

→ When calling STS:AssumeRole, the caller can pass a SESSION POLICY
→ This FURTHER restricts the permissions of the assumed role for this specific session

Assumed Role Permissions = INTERSECTION of (Role IAM Policy ∩ Session Policy)

Example:
→ CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEurope allows GetObject on products/* AND pricing/*
→ Europe's Glue job only needs products/* for this specific job run
→ Glue passes a session policy that only allows GetObject on products/*
→ Result: This session can ONLY access products/* — even though the role allows pricing/* too

Why use Session Policies?

BenefitExplanation
Principle of least privilege per sessionDifferent jobs need different subsets of the role's permissions
Defense against compromised sessionIf temporary credentials are stolen, they have minimal permissions
Audit claritySession policy shows EXACTLY what each session was scoped to

Session Policy Limits:
→ Maximum session policy size: 2048 characters (URL-encoded)
→ Session policy can ONLY restrict — it CANNOT grant permissions beyond the role's IAM policy
→ It acts as a CEILING, not a floor

MECHANIC 5 — ROLE CHAINING

→ What if Europe's Glue role assumes HQ's CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEurope role
→ And THAT role tries to assume ANOTHER role?

EuropeGlueETLRole (Account B)
    → assumes CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEurope (Account A)
        → attempts to assume AnotherRole (Account A or C)

→ This is called ROLE CHAINING
→ AWS ALLOWS role chaining but with restrictions:

RestrictionDetail
Maximum session durationChained role sessions are limited to 1 HOUR maximum (cannot extend)
Trust policyEach role in the chain must trust the PREVIOUS role
PermissionsEach hop can only NARROW permissions — not expand
CloudTrailEach AssumeRole call is logged separately

→ For ShopNow → Role chaining is NOT needed (one hop is sufficient)
→ Awareness is important for Project 40 where more complex trust patterns are explored

📈 SCALABILITY CONSIDERATIONS

→ Number of Cross-Account Roles Scaling

  • No hard limit on cross-account roles per account
  • Best practice: One role per partner account per access pattern
  • Example: CrossAccountReader-ShopNowEurope, CrossAccountReader-ShopNowUS, CrossAccountReader-PartnerXYZ
  • If you have 50 partner accounts → 50 cross-account roles → manageable
  • If you have 500+ partner accounts → Use AWS Organizations with organizational conditions (Project 54)

→ STS API Scaling

  • STS AssumeRole: No hard rate limit — designed for high-volume use
  • Each AssumeRole call takes ~100-200ms
  • Temporary credentials cached → no need to call AssumeRole for every S3 request
  • One AssumeRole → use credentials for up to 12 hours of S3 access

→ Bucket Policy Size Scaling

  • Maximum bucket policy size: 20 KB
  • Each cross-account principal statement adds ~200-500 bytes
  • With 10 partner accounts → ~5 KB → within limits
  • With 50+ partner accounts → approaching limit
  • Solution: Use IAM conditions with aws:PrincipalOrgID (Project 54) instead of listing every account

→ KMS Key Policy Scaling

  • Maximum key policy size: 32 KB
  • Each cross-account principal adds ~200 bytes
  • Practical limit: ~100 cross-account principals per key
  • For more → Use KMS Grants instead of key policy entries (grants have no policy size limit)

→ Cross-Region Access Scaling

  • Data transfer costs scale linearly with volume
  • For high-volume cross-region access → S3 Replication (Project 30) is more cost-effective
  • For low-volume ad-hoc queries → Cross-account direct access is fine
  • Transfer Acceleration can improve speed for large cross-region transfers

→ Multi-Account Architecture Scaling

  • 2-5 accounts → Individual cross-account roles work fine
  • 5-20 accounts → Use naming conventions and standardized role templates
  • 20-100 accounts → Use AWS Organizations + SCPs + organizational conditions (Project 54)
  • 100+ accounts → Use AWS RAM (Resource Access Manager) for sharing + Lake Formation (Project 42)

🎯 KEY TAKEAWAY

→ Cross-account access is the MOST CRITICAL skill for enterprise AWS — real organizations have multiple accounts
→ AWS accounts are the STRONGEST isolation boundary — cross-account access must be EXPLICITLY granted from BOTH sides
→ Two methods exist: Bucket Policy (resource-based) and Role Assumption (identity-based) — Role Assumption is preferred for KMS-encrypted resources
→ The trust chain requires DUAL authorization: Trust Policy in Account A + IAM Policy in Account B — both must agree
→ External ID prevents confused deputy attacks — always use it for cross-account role assumption
→ For SSE-KMS encrypted S3 objects → THREE policies must align: Bucket Policy + KMS Key Policy + IAM Policy
→ EXPLICIT DENY in bucket policy is the strongest guardrail — overrides any Allow in any other policy
→ Temporary credentials from STS auto-expire — fundamentally more secure than permanent access keys
→ CloudTrail in BOTH accounts provides the complete audit trail of cross-account activity
→ Cross-region cross-account access incurs data transfer costs — evaluate replication vs direct access
